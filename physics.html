Kinematics

Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes the motion of points, bodies, and systems of bodies without considering the forces that cause the motion.

ðŸ“š Chapter Overview

Kinematics deals with concepts like displacement, velocity, acceleration, and their relationships through mathematical equations.

Basic Concepts

Distance vs Displacement

Distance: Total path length (scalar)

Displacement: Shortest path between points (vector)

Speed vs Velocity

Speed: Rate of change of distance (scalar)

Velocity: Rate of change of displacement (vector)

Key Equations

Differential Form

  • Velocity: \( v = \frac{dx}{dt} \)
  • Acceleration: \( a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \)
  • Jerk: \( j = \frac{da}{dt} = \frac{d^3x}{dt^3} \)

Equations of Motion (Constant Acceleration)

\( v = u + at \)

Final velocity

\( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)

Displacement

\( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)

Velocity-position relation

\( s = \frac{u + v}{2}t \)

Motion in Different Dimensions

1D Motion (Rectilinear)

Motion along a straight line

  • Equations of motion apply directly
  • Positive and negative directions
  • Examples: Free fall, motion on inclined plane

2D Motion (Projectile)

Motion under constant acceleration (gravity)

Horizontal Motion

\( x = u_xt \)

\( v_x = u_x \) (constant)

Vertical Motion

\( y = u_yt - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \)

\( v_y = u_y - gt \)

Key Results:

  • Time of flight: \( T = \frac{2u\sin\theta}{g} \)
  • Maximum height: \( H = \frac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} \)
  • Range: \( R = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g} \)

Circular Motion

Angular Quantities

\( \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \)

\( \alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} \)

Linear Relations

\( v = r\omega \)

\( a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2 \)

Relative Motion

The motion of an object as observed from different reference frames

Position

\( \vec{r}_{AB} = \vec{r}_{AC} + \vec{r}_{CB} \)

Velocity

\( \vec{v}_{AB} = \vec{v}_{AC} + \vec{v}_{CB} \)

Examples:

  • Boats crossing rivers
  • Airplanes with wind
  • Objects on moving platforms

Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's laws describe the relationship between a body and the forces acting upon it, and its motion in response to those forces. These three fundamental laws form the foundation of classical mechanics.

ðŸ“š Chapter Overview

Newton's Laws explain how objects move when forces act upon them, from everyday motion to planetary movements.

The Three Laws of Motion

First Law

Law of Inertia

"An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force."

Key Concept:

Inertia - resistance to change in motion

Second Law

Law of Acceleration

"The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass."

Formula:

\( \vec{F} = m\vec{a} \)

Third Law

Action-Reaction Law

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Key Concept:

Forces always occur in pairs

Detailed Explanation

First Law: Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. The mass of an object is a quantitative measure of its inertia.

Examples:

  • Passengers lurch forward when a bus stops suddenly
  • Tablecloth trick - pulling tablecloth without moving dishes
  • Car continuing to move after engine is turned off

Mathematical Expression:

If \( \sum \vec{F} = 0 \), then \( \vec{v} = \text{constant} \)

Second Law: Force and Acceleration

The net force acting on an object equals the rate of change of its momentum.

Vector Form:

\( \vec{F} = m\vec{a} \)

Force = mass Ã— acceleration

Component Form:

\( F_x = ma_x \), \( F_y = ma_y \), \( F_z = ma_z \)

Key Points:

  • Acceleration is in the direction of net force
  • Mass measures inertia (resistance to acceleration)
  • Force causes change in motion, not motion itself

Third Law: Action and Reaction

Forces always occur in pairs - if object A exerts a force on object B, then object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

Mathematical Expression:

\( \vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA} \)

Important Notes:

  • Forces act on different objects
  • Forces are of same type
  • Forces are simultaneous

Examples:

  • Rocket propulsion
  • Walking on ground
  • Swimming in water
  • Book resting on table

Free Body Diagrams (FBD)

Free Body Diagrams are essential tools for solving problems using Newton's Laws. They show all forces acting on a single object.

Steps to Draw FBD:

  1. Isolate the object of interest
  2. Identify all forces acting on the object
  3. Draw arrows representing each force
  4. Label all forces clearly
  5. Choose coordinate system

Example: Block on Incline

Normal force perpendicular to surface

Friction parallel to surface

Heat & Temperature

Heat is the transfer of thermal energy between physical systems, while temperature is a physical quantity that expresses hot and cold.

Key Concepts

  • Specific heat capacity: \( Q = mc\Delta T \)
  • Latent heat: \( Q = mL \)
  • Thermal expansion: \( \Delta L = \alpha L \Delta T \)

Thermodynamics Laws

The laws of thermodynamics define fundamental physical quantities that characterize thermodynamic systems.

The Four Laws

  1. Zeroth Law: If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other.
  2. First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. \( \Delta U = Q - W \)
  3. Second Law: The entropy of an isolated system never decreases.
  4. Third Law: The entropy of a system approaches a constant value as the temperature approaches absolute zero.

Electrostatics

Electrostatics is the branch of physics that studies electric charges at rest.

Coulomb's Law

\( F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \)

Where F is the force between charges, k is Coulomb's constant, qâ‚ and qâ‚‚ are the charges, and r is the distance between them.

Electric Field

\( E = \frac{F}{q} = k \frac{Q}{r^2} \)

Current Electricity

Current electricity is the flow of electric charge through a conductor.

Ohm's Law

\( V = IR \)

Where V is voltage, I is current, and R is resistance.

Power in Electrical Circuits

\( P = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \)
