PROBLEM STATEMENT

A block of mass 2 kg is placed on a rough inclined plane with angle 37°. The coefficient of friction is 0.5. Will the block slide down? If yes, find its acceleration.

Given Values

Mass, m = 2 kg

Angle, θ = 37°

μ = 0.5

g = 10 m/s²

Required

• Will block slide?

• If yes, acceleration = ?

This problem tests understanding of friction and inclined plane mechanics

Understanding the Problem

Key Concept

When an object is placed on an inclined plane, two forces compete:

• Component of gravity pulling down = mg sinθ

• Maximum static friction holding back = μ mg cosθ

Decision Making

The block will slide only if:

mg sinθ > μ mg cosθ

Which simplifies to:

tanθ > μ

Our Specific Case

We need to compare:

• tan(37°) = ?

• μ = 0.5

If tan(37°) > 0.5, the block will slide. Otherwise, it stays at rest.

Step 1: Will the Block Slide?

Calculate tanθ

For angle θ = 37°, we know:

sin37° = 0.6, cos37° = 0.8

Therefore:

tan37° = sin37°/cos37° = 0.6/0.8 = 0.75

Compare with μ

We have:

tanθ = 0.75 and μ = 0.5

Since 0.75 > 0.5

✓ YES, the block will slide down

Explanation

The component of gravity pulling the block down (mg sinθ) is stronger than the maximum static friction (μ mg cosθ) that can hold it back.

This is because tanθ > μ, meaning the angle is steep enough to overcome friction.

Step 2: Free Body Diagram Analysis

Forces Acting on Block

Weight (mg) = 2 × 10 = 20 N downward

Normal Force (N) = mg cosθ perpendicular to plane

Friction (f) = μN opposing motion

Force Components

• Down the plane: mg sinθ

• Perpendicular: mg cosθ

• Friction: μ mg cosθ up the plane

Visual Representation

Inclined Plane (37°)
Block

↓ mg sinθ (down plane)

↑ μ mg cosθ (friction)

↙ mg (gravity)

Since the block is sliding, we use kinetic friction which equals μN

Step 3: Calculate Net Force

Force Down the Plane

Component of gravity pulling block down:

F_down = mg sinθ

= 2 × 10 × sin37° = 20 × 0.6 = 12 N

Friction Force Opposing

Kinetic friction acting up the plane:

f = μ mg cosθ

= 0.5 × 2 × 10 × cos37° = 10 × 0.8 = 8 N

Net Force

Net force causing acceleration:

F_net = F_down - f

= 12 N - 8 N = 4 N

This net force acts down the inclined plane

Step 4: Find Acceleration

Using Newton's Second Law

According to Newton's second law:

F_net = m × a

Where:

F_net = 4 N (from previous step)

m = 2 kg

Solve for Acceleration

4 = 2 × a

a = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 m/s²

Final Answer

Acceleration = 2 m/s²

The block slides down the incline with constant acceleration of 2 m/s²

Step 5: Verification

Check Using General Formula

For object sliding down rough incline:

a = g(sinθ - μ cosθ)

= 10 × (sin37° - 0.5 × cos37°)

= 10 × (0.6 - 0.5 × 0.8)

= 10 × (0.6 - 0.4) = 10 × 0.2 = 2 m/s²

✓ Matches our step-by-step calculation

Physical Significance

The acceleration is positive (2 m/s²), confirming the block slides down.

If we got zero or negative acceleration, it would mean the block doesn't move or moves up, which would be physically impossible in this scenario.

Complete Solution Summary

FINAL ANSWERS

Will it slide?

YES

Acceleration

2 m/s²

Key Learning Points

• To check if block slides: Compare tanθ with μ

• If tanθ > μ, block slides down

• Net force = mg sinθ - μ mg cosθ

• Acceleration = g(sinθ - μ cosθ)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

• Using static friction when object is moving

• Forgetting to resolve forces properly

• Using wrong angle in trigonometric functions

• Not verifying if sliding occurs first
