What is Circular Motion?

Circular Motion is the movement of an object along the circumference of a circle or rotation along a circular path.

Even with constant speed, it involves acceleration due to continuous change in direction.

Key Concept:

Acceleration is always directed towards the center (centripetal acceleration)

JEE Significance: 2-3 questions every year. Essential for mechanics section.

Real-world examples:

Satellites

Turning Cars

Atomic Models

Types of Circular Motion

Uniform Circular Motion

✓ Constant speed

✓ Constant angular velocity

✓ Only centripetal acceleration

✓ Tangential acceleration = 0

Example: Earth's revolution

Non-uniform Circular Motion

✓ Speed changes with time

✓ Angular velocity not constant

✓ Both centripetal & tangential acceleration

✓ Net acceleration not towards center

Example: Roller coaster

JEE Memory Tip: "Uniform = Constant speed, changing direction" | "Non-uniform = Changing speed and direction"

Centripetal Force

Centripetal force is the net force acting towards the center that causes circular motion.

Centripetal Force Formula

Fc = mv²/r = mω²r

Always directed towards the center

Important Points

✓ Not a new force

✓ Role played by existing forces

✓ Perpendicular to velocity

✓ Does zero work

Common Sources

✓ Tension (string)

✓ Friction (roads)

✓ Normal reaction

✓ Gravitational force

Banking of Roads

Banking Angle Formula

tanθ = v²/rg

Ideal banking angle for frictionless turning

Concept

✓ Outer edge raised

✓ Component of normal reaction provides centripetal force

✓ Eliminates need for friction at ideal speed

Applications

✓ Highway curves

✓ Race tracks

✓ Railway tracks

Vertical Circular Motion

Critical Points

✓ Highest point: vmin = √(rg)

✓ Lowest point: Maximum tension

✓ Complete loop: v ≥ √(5gr) at bottom

✓ Energy conservation applies

Tension Analysis

✓ Top: T = mv²/r - mg

✓ Bottom: T = mv²/r + mg

✓ Horizontal: T = mv²/r

✓ Energy: vbottom² = vtop² + 4gr

JEE Strategy: For complete vertical loop, minimum speed at bottom = √(5gr) and at top = √(gr)

Conical Pendulum

Time Period Formula

T = 2π√(Lcosθ/g)

Independent of mass of bob

Key Relationships

✓ Tension T = mg/cosθ

✓ Angular speed ω = √(g/Lcosθ)

✓ Radius r = Lsinθ

✓ Speed v = √(rg tanθ)

Characteristics

✓ Bob moves in horizontal circle

✓ String traces conical surface

✓ Uniform circular motion

✓ Constant time period

Centrifugal vs Centripetal

Centrifugal Force

✓ Pseudo force

✓ Appears in rotating frames

✓ Directed away from center

✓ Fcf = mv²/r

Example: Turning car

Centripetal Force

✓ Real force

✓ Acts in inertial frames

✓ Directed toward center

✓ Fc = mv²/r

Example: String tension

JEE Note: Centrifugal force is used in non-inertial frames to apply Newton's laws. In inertial frames, only centripetal force exists.

JEE Example Problem 1

JEE Main 2021 Question:

A stone of mass 0.5 kg is attached to a string of length 2 m and whirled in a horizontal circle. If the string can withstand a maximum tension of 100 N, what is the maximum speed with which the stone can be whirled?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify centripetal force

Tension provides centripetal force: T = mv²/r

Step 2: Apply maximum tension

Tmax = mv²max/r

100 = (0.5 × v²max) / 2

Step 3: Solve for maximum speed

100 = 0.25 × v²max

v²max = 400

vmax = 20 m/s

JEE Example Problem 2

JEE Advanced 2019 Question:

A small block slides from rest from the top of a frictionless hemispherical bowl of radius R. Find the angle θ at which the block will leave the surface of the bowl.

Solution Approach:

Step 1: Energy conservation

mgR = mgRcosθ + ½mv²

v² = 2gR(1 - cosθ)

Step 2: Circular motion at leaving point

When block leaves, normal reaction becomes zero

mgcosθ = mv²/R

Step 3: Solve for θ

mgcosθ = m[2gR(1 - cosθ)]/R

cosθ = 2(1 - cosθ)

cosθ = 2/3 ⇒ θ = cos⁻¹(2/3)
