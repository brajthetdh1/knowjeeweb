Electromagnetic Induction

The phenomenon of generating EMF by changing magnetic flux

When a conductor is placed in a changing magnetic field, an electromotive force (EMF) is induced, which causes a current to flow if the circuit is closed.

Φ = B ⋅ A = BA cosθ

Magnetic flux (Φ) depends on magnetic field strength (B), area (A), and angle (θ) between field and area vector.

JEE Tip: This topic frequently appears in both JEE Main and Advanced. Focus on conceptual understanding and problem-solving speed.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's law states that the induced EMF in a coil is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the coil.

ε = -N dΦB/dt

Where:

  • ε is the induced EMF (in volts)
  • N is the number of turns in the coil
  • dΦB/dt is the rate of change of magnetic flux
  • The negative sign indicates direction (Lenz's Law)

JEE Focus: Remember that induced EMF depends on the rate of change of flux, not the flux itself.

Lenz's Law

Lenz's law gives the direction of the induced current: The induced current will flow in a direction that opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.

"Nature abhors a change in flux"

This is the reason for the negative sign in Faraday's law.

Remember: If flux is increasing, induced current creates opposite field. If flux is decreasing, induced current creates same field.

JEE Strategy: Use the right-hand rule to determine current direction in Lenz's Law problems.

Problem 1: Calculating Induced EMF

A circular coil of 100 turns with a cross-sectional area of 0.01 m² is placed in a magnetic field of 0.1 T. If the magnetic field is reduced to zero in 0.1 seconds, what is the magnitude of the induced EMF?

Step 1: Identify known values

  • Number of turns, N = 100
  • Area, A = 0.01 m²
  • Initial magnetic field, B₁ = 0.1 T
  • Final magnetic field, B₂ = 0 T
  • Time interval, Δt = 0.1 s

Step 2: Calculate change in magnetic flux

Initial flux, Φ₁ = B₁ × A = 0.1 × 0.01 = 0.001 Wb

Final flux, Φ₂ = B₂ × A = 0 × 0.01 = 0 Wb

Change in flux, ΔΦ = Φ₂ - Φ₁ = 0 - 0.001 = -0.001 Wb

Step 3: Apply Faraday's Law

According to Faraday's Law:

|ε| = N × |ΔΦ/Δt|

|ε| = 100 × |(-0.001) / 0.1|

|ε| = 100 × 0.01

|ε| = 1.0 V

Step 4: Final Answer

The magnitude of induced EMF is 1.0 V

Note: The negative sign in Faraday's law indicates direction (Lenz's Law), but the question asks for magnitude, so we take the absolute value.

Time Management: This type of problem should take 2-3 minutes in JEE.

Problem 2: Lenz's Law Application

A magnet is falling through a vertical copper pipe. Explain why the magnet falls slower than it would in free fall, using Lenz's law.

Step 1: Understand the setup

As the magnet falls through the copper pipe, the magnetic flux through sections of the pipe changes with time.

N S

Step 2: Apply Faraday's Law

The changing magnetic flux induces eddy currents in the copper pipe according to Faraday's Law.

ε = -dΦ/dt

Step 3: Apply Lenz's Law

According to Lenz's Law, the induced eddy currents will create magnetic fields that oppose the change causing them.

The falling magnet causes increasing downward flux below it and decreasing downward flux above it.

Step 4: Determine the effect

The induced currents create magnetic fields that:

  • Below the magnet: Create an upward magnetic field (opposing increasing downward flux)
  • Above the magnet: Create a downward magnetic field (opposing decreasing downward flux)

Both effects result in an upward force on the magnet, opposing its motion.

Step 5: Final Answer

The magnet falls slower due to electromagnetic braking caused by eddy currents induced in the pipe, which create opposing magnetic fields according to Lenz's Law.

JEE Insight: This is a classic demonstration of Lenz's Law and appears frequently in JEE conceptual questions.

Summary: Electromagnetic Induction

Key Concepts

  • Electromagnetic induction produces EMF in a changing magnetic field
  • Faraday's Law: ε = -N dΦ/dt
  • Lenz's Law determines direction of induced current
  • Magnetic flux: Φ = BA cosθ
  • Flux changes if B, A, or θ changes

Problem Solving Approach

  • Identify changing flux (B, A, or θ)
  • Calculate rate of change of flux
  • Apply Faraday's Law for EMF magnitude
  • Use Lenz's Law for current direction
  • Consider practical applications (generators, transformers)
