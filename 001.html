Introduction to Special Relativity

Special Theory of Relativity, published by Albert Einstein in 1905, revolutionized our understanding of space, time, and motion.

Core Idea:

The laws of physics are the same for all observers in uniform motion, and the speed of light is constant for all observers.

This theory becomes significant when objects move at speeds comparable to the speed of light.

JEE Significance: Essential for JEE Advanced, appears in modern physics section with 1-2 questions.

Key Concepts:

Postulates of Special Relativity

Einstein built his theory on two fundamental postulates:

First Postulate

✓ Principle of Relativity

✓ Laws of physics are identical in all inertial frames

✓ No preferred reference frame exists

✓ Physical laws have the same form for all observers

Second Postulate

✓ Constancy of Light Speed

✓ Speed of light in vacuum is constant

✓ Independent of source or observer motion

✓ c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s for all observers

"The most incomprehensible thing about the universe is that it is comprehensible." - Albert Einstein

Time Dilation

Moving clocks run slower compared to stationary clocks. This is one of the most fascinating consequences of special relativity.

Δt = Δt₀ / √(1 - v²/c²)

Where:

Δt = Time interval in moving frame (Dilated time)

Δt₀ = Proper time (Time in rest frame)

v = Relative velocity

c = Speed of light (3×10⁸ m/s)

Key Points:

Proper time is measured in the frame where events occur at same position

Time dilation becomes significant when v approaches c

This is experimentally verified with atomic clocks

JEE Strategy: Always identify which frame measures proper time. Proper time is the shortest possible time interval.

Length Contraction

Objects in motion appear shorter in the direction of motion compared to their rest length.

L = L₀ √(1 - v²/c²)

Proper Length

L₀

Length measured in object's rest frame

Maximum length possible

Contracted Length

L

Length measured from moving frame

Always less than proper length

Important: Length contraction occurs only in the direction of motion. Dimensions perpendicular to motion remain unchanged.

Relativistic Momentum & Mass

In relativity, momentum and mass also transform at high speeds.

Relativistic Momentum

p = m₀v / √(1 - v²/c²)

Relativistic Mass

m = m₀ / √(1 - v²/c²)

Variables:

p = Relativistic momentum

m = Relativistic mass

m₀ = Rest mass

v = Velocity

c = Speed of light

Key Insights:

As v → c, m → ∞

Infinite energy required to reach c

Explains why c is speed limit

Rest mass m₀ is invariant

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein's most famous equation shows the fundamental relationship between mass and energy.

E = mc²

Total Energy:

E = m₀c² / √(1 - v²/c²)

Kinetic Energy: KE = (m - m₀)c²

Rest Energy: E₀ = m₀c²

Applications:

Nuclear reactions

Particle physics

Cosmology

GPS satellite corrections

"It followed from the special theory of relativity that mass and energy are both but different manifestations of the same thing." - Albert Einstein

Lorentz Transformation

Lorentz transformation equations relate space and time coordinates between different inertial frames.

Transformation Equations

x' = γ(x - vt)

y' = y

z' = z

t' = γ(t - vx/c²)

Where:

γ = 1/√(1 - v²/c²) (Lorentz factor)

(x, y, z, t) = Coordinates in S frame

(x', y', z', t') = Coordinates in S' frame

v = Relative velocity between frames

Properties:

Reduces to Galilean transform when v << c

Preserves speed of light

Mixes space and time coordinates

Fundamental to special relativity

Relativistic Velocity Addition

In relativity, velocities don't add simply. The correct formula ensures no object exceeds light speed.

u = (u' + v) / (1 + u'v/c²)

Variables

u = Velocity in S frame

u' = Velocity in S' frame

v = Relative velocity between frames

c = Speed of light

Special Cases

If u' = c, then u = c

If v << c, reduces to u ≈ u' + v

Always ensures u ≤ c

JEE Application: Used in problems involving relative motion of particles at relativistic speeds.

JEE Example Problem 1

JEE Advanced 2018 Question:

A spaceship of proper length 100 m moves at speed 0.8c relative to Earth. What length does an Earth observer measure?

Solution:

Given:

Proper length L₀ = 100 m

Velocity v = 0.8c

γ = 1/√(1 - v²/c²) = 1/√(1 - 0.64) = 1/√0.36 = 1/0.6 = 5/3

Length Contraction Formula:

L = L₀ √(1 - v²/c²) = L₀ / γ

L = 100 × √(1 - 0.64) = 100 × √0.36 = 100 × 0.6 = 60 m

Answer: 60 meters

JEE Example Problem 2

JEE Advanced 2020 Question:

A muon is created at altitude 10 km in Earth's atmosphere with speed 0.998c. If its proper lifetime is 2.2 μs, will it reach Earth's surface?

Solution Approach:

From Earth's frame:

Time dilation: Δt = Δt₀ / √(1 - v²/c²)

γ = 1/√(1 - 0.998²) = 1/√(1 - 0.996) = 1/√0.004 ≈ 15.81

Lifetime in Earth frame: Δt = 2.2 × 15.81 ≈ 34.78 μs

Distance traveled: d = v × Δt = 0.998c × 34.78μs ≈ 10.4 km

From muon's frame:

Length contraction: L = L₀ / γ = 10 / 15.81 ≈ 0.632 km

Time to travel: t = L/v = 0.632/(0.998c) ≈ 2.11 μs

Conclusion: Yes, it reaches surface (2.11 μs < 2.2 μs)

Experimental Verification

Muon Decay

More muons reach Earth's surface than classical physics predicts due to time dilation

Atomic Clocks

Clocks on airplanes run slower than ground clocks, verifying time dilation

GPS Systems

GPS satellites require relativistic corrections for accurate positioning

Particle Accelerators

Particles moving at relativistic speeds show increased mass and lifetime

Modern physics relies heavily on special relativity. It's not just theory - it's experimentally verified technology!
