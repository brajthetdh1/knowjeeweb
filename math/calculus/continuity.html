Continuity of Functions

Step 1: Continuity at a Point

A function \( f(x) \) is continuous at \( x = a \) if:

\( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \)

Equivalently, three conditions must hold:

  • \( f(a) \) is defined
  • \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \) exists
  • \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \)

Example:

\( f(x) = 3x + 2 \) is continuous at \( x = 1 \) since \( \lim_{x \to 1} (3x+2) = 3(1)+2 = 5 = f(1) \)

Step 2: Continuity from Left and Right

Function is continuous from the right at \( x = a \) if:

\( \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a) \)

Function is continuous from the left at \( x = a \) if:

\( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = f(a) \)

Continuous at a point requires both left and right limits to be equal to \( f(a) \).

Step 3: Continuity on an Interval

Open interval \( (a,b) \): continuous at every point inside the interval.

Closed interval \( [a,b] \): continuous at every point inside, right-continuous at \( a \), left-continuous at \( b \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2 \) is continuous on \( [0,2] \)

Step 4: Types of Discontinuity

  • Removable: \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \) exists, but \( f(a) \) is undefined or \( f(a) \neq \lim f(x) \)
  • Jump: \( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \neq \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) \)
  • Infinite / Essential: \( f(x) \to \infty \) or oscillates infinitely near \( x = a \)

Example (Jump):

\( f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x < 0 \\ 2 & x \ge 0 \end{cases} \) has jump at \( x = 0 \)

Step 5: Algebra of Continuous Functions

Sum, difference, product, and quotient (denominator ≠ 0) of continuous functions are continuous.

Example:

If \( f(x) = x^2 \) and \( g(x) = \sin x \), then \( f(x) + g(x) \) and \( f(x)g(x) \) are continuous everywhere.

Step 6: Important Theorems

  • Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT): If \( f \) continuous on \( [a,b] \) and \( f(a)\neq f(b) \), then for any \( k \) between \( f(a) \) and \( f(b) \), \( \exists c \in (a,b) \) such that \( f(c) = k \)
  • Continuous functions have limits at every point in their domain.

Techniques and Concepts of Continuity

Step 1: Continuity at a Point

A function \( f(x) \) is continuous at \( x = a \) if:

\( \displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \)

Example:

\( f(x) = 2x + 3, \quad a = 1 \Rightarrow f(1) = 5, \; \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 5 \)

Step 2: Left and Right Continuity

Right-continuity at \( x = a \) : \( \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a) \)

Left-continuity at \( x = a \) : \( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = f(a) \)

Example:

\( f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2, & x<1 \\ 2, & x \ge 1 \end{cases} \)

Here, left limit = 1, right limit = 2 → jump discontinuity at x = 1

Step 3: Continuity on Interval

Continuous on open interval \( (a,b) \) if continuous at every point inside.

Continuous on closed interval \( [a,b] \) if continuous inside, right-continuous at \( a \) and left-continuous at \( b \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^3 \quad \text{is continuous on } [0,2] \)

Step 4: Types of Discontinuity

  • Removable: limit exists but \( f(a) \) undefined or different. \( f(x) = \frac{x^2-1}{x-1}, x\neq1 \)
  • Jump: left and right limits exist but unequal. \( f(x) = \begin{cases}1,&x<0\\2,&x\ge0\end{cases} \)
  • Infinite/Essential: function goes to infinity or oscillates. \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x}, x \to 0 \)

Step 5: Algebra of Continuous Functions

Sum, difference, product, quotient (denominator ≠ 0) of continuous functions are continuous.

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2, g(x) = \sin x \Rightarrow f+g = x^2 + \sin x, \; fg = x^2 \sin x \)

Step 6: Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)

If \( f \) is continuous on \( [a,b] \) and \( f(a)\neq f(b) \), then for any \( k \in (f(a),f(b)) \), there exists \( c \in (a,b) \) such that \( f(c) = k \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^3 - x - 2 \text{ on } [1,2] \Rightarrow f(1)=-2, f(2)=4 \Rightarrow \exists c \in (1,2): f(c)=0 \)

Step 7: Important Notes

  • Polynomials, trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic functions are continuous in their domains.
  • Check left and right limits for piecewise functions.
  • Removable discontinuities can be fixed by redefining \( f(a) \).
  • IVT is very useful to check existence of roots.

More Examples

Step 1: Continuity at a Point

A function \( f(x) \) is continuous at \( x = a \) if \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2 + 3x + 2 \),
\( \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 1^2 + 3 \cdot 1 + 2 = 6 \)

Step 2: Continuity at Endpoints

Right-continuous at \( a \): \( \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a) \), Left-continuous at \( b \): \( \lim_{x \to b^-} f(x) = f(b) \)

Example:

\( f(x) = \sqrt{x} \) on \( [0,4] \),
Right-continuous at 0: \( \lim_{x \to 0^+} \sqrt{x} = 0 \),
Left-continuous at 4: \( \lim_{x \to 4^-} \sqrt{x} = 2 \)

Step 3: Composition of Continuous Functions

If \( g \) is continuous at \( a \) and \( f \) is continuous at \( g(a) \), then \( f(g(x)) \) is continuous at \( x = a \).

Example:

\( f(x) = e^x, g(x) = \sin x \),
\( f(g(x)) = e^{\sin x} \) → continuous everywhere

Step 4: Algebra of Continuous Functions

If \( f \) and \( g \) are continuous at \( x = a \), then \( f+g, f-g, f \cdot g \) are continuous at \( a \), and \( f/g \) is continuous at \( a \) if \( g(a) \neq 0 \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2, g(x) = \cos x \),
\( f(x) + g(x) = x^2 + \cos x \) → continuous everywhere

Step 5: Types of Discontinuity

  • Removable: Limit exists but \( f(a) \neq \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \) or undefined, e.g. \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \) at \( x=1 \)
  • Jump: Left & right limits exist but not equal, e.g. \( f(x)=1 \) for \( x<0 \), \( f(x)=2 \) for \( x \ge 0 \)
  • Infinite: Function → ±∞ near a, e.g. \( f(x)=\frac{1}{x} \) at \( x=0 \)
  • Oscillatory: Limit does not exist, e.g. \( f(x) = \sin(1/x) \) at \( x=0 \)

Step 6: Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)

If \( f \) is continuous on \( [a,b] \), then it takes every value between \( f(a) \) and \( f(b) \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^3 - x - 1 \), on [1,2],
\( f(1)=-1, f(2)=5 \) → there exists \( c \in (1,2) \) with \( f(c)=0 \)

Step 7: Continuity in Piecewise Functions

Check boundary: \( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a) \)

Example:

\( f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2, & x \le 1 \\ 2x+1, & x > 1 \end{cases} \),
LHL = 1, RHL = 3, \( f(1)=1 \) → discontinuous at x=1

Step 8: Important Notes

  • Always check limits at boundary points for piecewise functions.
  • Polynomials, exponentials, logs, and standard trig functions are continuous everywhere.
  • Removable discontinuities can be “fixed” by redefining \( f(a) \).
  • One-sided limits are critical for solving JEE problems.

Differentiability Concepts

Step 1: Differentiability at a Point

A function \( f(x) \) is differentiable at \( x = a \) if the derivative exists:

\( f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \)

This represents the slope of the tangent to the curve at that point.

Step 2: One-Sided Derivatives

Right-hand derivative (RHD) and Left-hand derivative (LHD):

\( f'_+(a) = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}, \quad f'_-(a) = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \)

Function is differentiable at \( a \) if and only if \( f'_+(a) = f'_-(a) \).

Step 3: Differentiability Implies Continuity

If a function is differentiable at \( x = a \), it is automatically continuous there:

\( \text{Differentiable at } a \implies \text{Continuous at } a \)

However, continuity does not guarantee differentiability.

Step 4: Points of Non-Differentiability

  • Sharp corners or cusps (e.g., \( f(x) = |x| \) at \( x=0 \))
  • Vertical tangents (e.g., \( y = \sqrt[3]{x} \) at \( x=0 \))
  • Discontinuities (jump, removable, infinite)

Step 5: Higher-Order Derivatives

If the first derivative exists and is differentiable, we can define the second derivative:

\( f''(x) = \frac{d}{dx} [f'(x)] \)

Similarly, the \( n \)-th derivative can be defined if all previous derivatives exist.

Step 6: Chain Rule for Differentiability

If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \) are differentiable, then the composition is differentiable:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \)

Step 7: Important Notes

  • Every differentiable function is continuous, but not vice versa.
  • Check one-sided derivatives for piecewise functions.
  • Polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, trigonometric functions are differentiable in their domains.
  • Points of non-differentiability include cusps, corners, vertical tangents, and discontinuities.

Advanced Differentiability Concepts

Step 1: Piecewise Functions

To check differentiability at a point \( x=a \) for piecewise functions:

  • Ensure continuity at \( x=a \).
  • Check that left-hand derivative (LHD) equals right-hand derivative (RHD) at \( x=a \).

Example:

\( f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2, & x \le 1 \\ 2x-1, & x > 1 \end{cases} \)

Check at \( x=1 \):

\( f'_-(1) = 2(1) = 2, \quad f'_+(1) = 2 \implies f \text{ is differentiable at } x=1 \)

Step 2: Higher-Order Derivatives

Repeated derivatives are denoted as:

\( f''(x) = \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}, \quad f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{d^n y}{dx^n} \)

Example:

\( f(x) = x^3 \implies f'(x) = 3x^2, \quad f''(x) = 6x, \quad f^{(3)}(x) = 6 \)

Step 3: Trigonometric Functions

Sine, cosine, tangent and their compositions are differentiable in their domains:

\( \frac{d}{dx} \sin x = \cos x, \quad \frac{d}{dx} \cos x = -\sin x, \quad \frac{d}{dx} \tan x = \sec^2 x \)

Example:

\( f(x) = \sin(2x) \implies f'(x) = 2 \cos(2x) \)

Step 4: Implicit Differentiation

Used when \( y \) is defined implicitly by an equation \( F(x,y)=0 \).

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = - \frac{F_x}{F_y} \)

Example:

\( x^2 + y^2 = 25 \implies 2x + 2y \frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y} \)

Step 5: Logarithmic Differentiation

Useful when differentiating functions like \( y = [f(x)]^{g(x)} \).

\( \ln y = g(x) \ln f(x) \implies \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = g'(x)\ln f(x) + g(x)\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \)

Example:

\( y = x^x \implies \ln y = x \ln x \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = x^x (1 + \ln x) \)

Step 6: Exponential & Logarithmic Functions

Functions like \( e^x, a^x, \ln x \) are differentiable in their domains:

\( \frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x, \quad \frac{d}{dx} a^x = a^x \ln a, \quad \frac{d}{dx} \ln x = \frac{1}{x} \)

Step 7: Important Notes

  • Check differentiability carefully at points of piecewise definition.
  • Implicit differentiation helps for curves not solved explicitly for \( y \).
  • Logarithmic differentiation is useful for variable powers.
  • Remember: differentiable ⇒ continuous, but continuous ⇏ differentiable.

Key Properties of Differentiable Functions

Step 1: Differentiability ⇒ Continuity

If a function \( f(x) \) is differentiable at \( x = a \), then it is continuous at \( x = a \).

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2 \implies f'(x) = 2x \)
\( f \text{ is differentiable everywhere } \implies f \text{ is continuous everywhere} \)

Step 2: Sum & Difference Rule

If \( f(x) \) and \( g(x) \) are differentiable at \( x \), then:

\( (f+g)' = f' + g', \quad (f-g)' = f' - g' \)

Example:

\( f(x)=x^2, g(x)=3x \implies (f+g)' = 2x + 3 \)

Step 3: Product Rule

If \( f(x) \) and \( g(x) \) are differentiable at \( x \), then:

\( (fg)' = f'g + fg' \)

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2, g(x) = \sin x \implies (fg)' = 2x \sin x + x^2 \cos x \)

Step 4: Quotient Rule

If \( f(x) \) and \( g(x) \) are differentiable at \( x \) and \( g(x) \neq 0 \), then:

\( \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} \)

Example:

\( f(x) = x^2, g(x) = x+1 \implies \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{2x(x+1) - x^2}{(x+1)^2} = \frac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2} \)

Step 5: Chain Rule

If \( y = f(u) \) and \( u = g(x) \) are differentiable, then:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \)

Example:

\( y = \sin(x^2) \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x = 2x \cos(x^2) \)

Step 6: Constant Multiple Rule

If \( f(x) \) is differentiable and \( c \) is constant:

\( (c f)' = c f' \)

Example:

\( f(x) = x^3, c = 5 \implies (5x^3)' = 5 \cdot 3x^2 = 15x^2 \)

Step 7: Important Notes

  • Sum, difference, product, quotient, and chain rules allow differentiation of complex functions built from simpler differentiable functions.
  • Differentiability at a point implies continuity, but continuity alone does not guarantee differentiability.
  • For JEE, always check domain restrictions (like division by zero) before applying rules.
