Introduction to Doppler Effect

The Doppler Effect describes how the observed frequency of a wave changes when there is relative motion between the source and observer.

Core Concept:

When source and observer move closer, frequency increases. When they move apart, frequency decreases.

Discovered by Austrian physicist Christian Doppler in 1842 through sound wave experiments.

JEE Significance: 2-3 questions every year. Essential for both JEE Main and Advanced.

Applies to all types of waves:

Sound

Light

Radio

Doppler Effect in Sound Waves

Sound waves are mechanical waves that require a medium. The Doppler effect in sound is what we experience daily with moving vehicles.

Source Approaching

✓ Wavefronts compress in direction of motion

✓ Wavelength decreases (λ' < λ)

✓ Frequency increases (f' > f)

✓ Higher pitch perceived

Example: Ambulance approaching

Source Receding

✓ Wavefronts stretch behind the source

✓ Wavelength increases (λ' > λ)

✓ Frequency decreases (f' < f)

✓ Lower pitch perceived

Example: Train moving away

JEE Memory Tip: "Approaching = Squeezed waves = Higher frequency" | "Receding = Stretched waves = Lower frequency"

Mathematical Derivation

Let's derive the general formula step by step:

Case: Source Moving, Observer Stationary

Time period of source: T = 1/f

Distance covered by source in one time period: vₛT

Apparent wavelength: λ' = (v - vₛ)T

Apparent frequency: f' = v/λ' = v/[(v - vₛ)T]

Since T = 1/f ⇒ f' = f × [v/(v - vₛ)]

Key Variables:

v = Speed of sound in medium (m/s)

vₛ = Velocity of source (m/s)

v₀ = Velocity of observer (m/s)

f = Actual frequency (Hz)

f' = Apparent frequency (Hz)

Speed of Sound:

In air at 20°C: 343 m/s

In water: 1482 m/s

In steel: 5960 m/s

JEE problems typically use 330-340 m/s

General Formula & Sign Convention

f' = f × (v ± v₀) / (v ∓ vₛ)

Observer Motion (v₀):

+

Observer moves toward source

-

Observer moves away from source

Source Motion (vₛ):

-

Source moves toward observer

+

Source moves away from observer

JEE Strategy: Always draw direction arrows and assign signs systematically to avoid errors!

Special Case: Source Moving, Observer Stationary

When only the source is moving and observer is stationary (v₀ = 0):

f' = f × v / (v ∓ vₛ)

Source Approaching

f' = f × v / (v - vₛ)

f' > f (Frequency increases)

Maximum when vₛ → v

Source Receding

f' = f × v / (v + vₛ)

f' < f (Frequency decreases)

Minimum when vₛ → ∞

Physical Limit: When vₛ ≥ v, shock waves form (supersonic speeds)

Special Case: Observer Moving, Source Stationary

When only the observer is moving and source is stationary (vₛ = 0):

f' = f × (v ± v₀) / v

Observer Approaching

f' = f × (v + v₀) / v

f' > f (Frequency increases)

Relative speed increases

Observer Receding

f' = f × (v - v₀) / v

f' < f (Frequency decreases)

Relative speed decreases

Key Insight: For observer motion, the effect is linear (v₀/v), while for source motion it's nonlinear (v/(v∓vₛ))

Both Source and Observer Moving

Moving Toward Each Other

f' = f × (v + v₀) / (v - vₛ)

✓ Maximum frequency increase

✓ Both effects reinforce each other

✓ f' significantly greater than f

Moving Away From Each Other

f' = f × (v - v₀) / (v + vₛ)

✓ Maximum frequency decrease

✓ Both effects reinforce each other

✓ f' significantly less than f

Same Direction (Source ahead)

f' = f × (v - v₀) / (v - vₛ)

Effect depends on relative speed

Same Direction (Observer ahead)

f' = f × (v + v₀) / (v + vₛ)

Effect depends on relative speed

Doppler Effect in Light Waves

For electromagnetic waves (light), the Doppler effect causes color shifts due to relative motion.

BLUESHIFT

Source moving toward observer

Wavelength decreases: λ' < λ

Frequency increases: f' > f

Spectrum shifts to blue/violet

Andromeda galaxy approaching

REDSHIFT

Source moving away from observer

Wavelength increases: λ' > λ

Frequency decreases: f' < f

Spectrum shifts to red

Most galaxies show redshift

Cosmological Significance: Hubble's discovery of universal redshift led to Big Bang theory and expanding universe concept.

Relativistic Doppler Effect

For objects moving at relativistic speeds (comparable to speed of light), we use Einstein's special relativity formulas.

f' = f × √[(1 - β) / (1 + β)]

where β = v/c

Variables:

f = Rest frequency

f' = Observed frequency

v = Relative velocity

c = Speed of light (3×10⁸ m/s)

β = v/c (dimensionless)

Applications:

Particle physics experiments

Cosmological measurements

GPS satellite corrections

Astrophysical observations

JEE Advanced: Relativistic Doppler Effect appears in advanced problems, especially in modern physics section.

JEE Example Problem 1

JEE Main 2021 Question:

A train moving at 20 m/s blows a whistle of frequency 500 Hz. A car is moving parallel to the train at 15 m/s in the same direction. Calculate the frequency heard by the car driver when:

(a) The car is ahead of the train

(b) The train is ahead of the car

Speed of sound = 340 m/s

Solution:

Part (a): Car ahead of train

Both moving same direction, source (train) approaching observer (car)

f' = f × (v - v₀) / (v - vₛ) = 500 × (340 - 15) / (340 - 20)

f' = 500 × 325 / 320 ≈ 507.8 Hz

Part (b): Train ahead of car

Both moving same direction, source receding from observer

f' = f × (v - v₀) / (v + vₛ) = 500 × (340 - 15) / (340 + 20)

f' = 500 × 325 / 360 ≈ 451.4 Hz

JEE Example Problem 2

JEE Advanced 2019 Question:

A sound source S of frequency 600 Hz is moving toward a wall with velocity 30 m/s. An observer O is between the source and wall, moving away from the source at 10 m/s. Find the beat frequency heard by the observer.

Speed of sound = 330 m/s

Solution Approach:

Step 1: Direct sound from source

Source approaching, observer receding

f₁ = f × (v - v₀) / (v - vₛ) = 600 × (330 - 10) / (330 - 30)

f₁ = 600 × 320 / 300 = 640 Hz

Step 2: Reflected sound from wall

Wall acts as stationary observer, then as stationary source

Frequency at wall: f_wall = f × v / (v - vₛ) = 600 × 330 / 300 = 660 Hz

Observer hears reflected sound: f₂ = f_wall × (v + v₀) / v = 660 × (330 + 10) / 330

f₂ = 660 × 340 / 330 = 680 Hz

Step 3: Beat frequency

Beat frequency = |f₂ - f₁| = |680 - 640| = 40 Hz

Applications & Real-World Uses

Police Radar Guns

Measures vehicle speed using microwave Doppler shift. Accuracy: ±1 km/h

Medical Ultrasound

Doppler echocardiography measures blood flow velocity in heart and vessels

Astronomy & Cosmology

Measures speeds of stars, galaxies. Redshift proves universe expansion

Weather Radar

Doppler weather radar tracks storm movement, precipitation velocity

Navigation Systems

GPS uses relativistic Doppler corrections for accurate positioning

Scientific Research
